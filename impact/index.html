How Impact works

Four phases, one arc

Every project follows the same arc — from a community need you’ve only just heard to lasting, measurable impact for the people it serves. Each phase has its tools and ends at a gate you clear with evidence, not optimism.

The guide

Learn the tools, then go build

Each tool is a living document, not a form you fill once. Tap a tool to see what it does and how one team — the BridgeReady financial-literacy project — actually used it. The fillable worksheets live in the Word field guide (see Resources).

The path warms as you go: cool steel at Discover, through gold and rust, to a green finish at Launch. Move forward only when the gate is cleared — track that under My Progress.

You pass a gate with evidence — not optimism

Gates aren’t self-checked. They’re checkpoints you walk through with your instructor, who pressure-tests your evidence rather than rubber-stamping it. A gate you pass on optimism is a gate that fails you later.

01 · Show

Bring the evidence — conversations, a test result, a real user’s own words — not a feeling that you’re ready.

02 · Defend

Walk your instructor through it out loud. If you can’t, the gate isn’t clear.

03 · Decide

Pass and proceed, or revise and return — with what must be true to pass next time.

Talk to the people you serve first. Test before you build. Show what’s real, say it out loud, and clear the gate.

Your instructor & studio team

Impact runs as a studio, not a lecture. Your instructor is your first call when you’re stuck and your sounding board before a gate — there to pressure-test your evidence, not hand you answers.

The weekly studio

Short standups, just-in-time skill lessons tied to the phase you’re in, and rotating critique of one team at a time. The shared craft — running a standup, a hard partner conversation, killing scope creep — is taught to everyone; the phase-specific tools you pull when you reach them.

The community you serve

Your real stakeholder isn’t the classroom — it’s the community partner whose problem you don’t own. Keep them informed, test with them, and share an update every two weeks. The project belongs to them long after the semester ends.

My Progress

Track your own project

Name your project, then work down each phase. Check off a gate’s criteria as you clear them — your progress saves automatically in this browser. A phase turns green when every box is ticked.
